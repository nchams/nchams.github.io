Locating the Skive Window in CGM Sensor-Wire Manufacturing

Turning raw production measurements into an automated detection algorithm and reports engineers actually use.

Role
Data analysis & algorithm development, embedded with the process and manufacturing engineering team
Domain
Medical-device manufacturing: sensor wires for continuous glucose monitors (CGM)
Stack
Python, pandas/NumPy, scikit-learn, TensorFlow/Keras, statsmodels (LOWESS), Matplotlib, Plotly, Excel automation (openpyxl, XlsxWriter)

The problem

Every CGM sensor begins as a wire, and on every wire there is a skive window: the region where material is removed to expose the sensing element before the wire is dip-coated with its functional layers.

During production, each wire is measured at 31 positions along its length, from 3 rotational angles, at every dip cycle: bare wire first, then again after each coating dip. That's nearly a hundred dimensional readings per wire per cycle, across every lot and fixture in a run.

The process and manufacturing engineers needed to know, for every wire: where exactly is the skive window, and how is the coating building up inside and around it? Answering that by eyeballing spreadsheets was slow, inconsistent between engineers, and impossible to scale to full production lots.

Cleaning production data that fights back

Real manufacturing data is messy in ways a textbook never covers. Before any analysis could be trusted, we built a cleaning pipeline that dealt with:

  • Duplicate uploads: the same measurement recorded more than once;
  • Mislabeled dip cycles: wires with two "dip 1" entries where the second was actually dip 2, resolved by sorting on timestamps and re-labeling within each wire's measurement history;
  • An unwieldy wide layout: 93 angle-by-position columns per row, reshaped into a tidy long format keyed by lot, fixture, wire, dip cycle, and angle so every downstream step could group and compare cleanly.

The detection algorithm, iterated with the engineers

The skive window shows up as a trough in the wire's thickness profile. The interesting part wasn't the first version of the detector. It was converging, over review sessions with the engineers, on a definition that matched what they considered correct on real wires:

  • Threshold clustering: flag every position within a tolerance band above the profile's minimum (excluding a known edge artifact at the first position), then keep the largest contiguous run;
  • LOWESS smoothing: fit a local regression through noisy profiles first, so single-point noise couldn't split or shift the detected window;
  • Center-out expansion: an alternative formulation that starts at the deepest point and grows the window outward while neighboring positions stay within a physical tolerance (in micrometers, or scaled to the profile's own variability);
  • Slope-based edge rules: after the engineers reviewed the shaded outputs against wires they knew, we encoded their boundary judgments as explicit rules that trim a window edge when the profile rises or falls faster than a set rate.

Each iteration was driven by the same loop: run the detector across the full dataset, put the results in front of the engineers in a form they could audit, and translate their disagreements into the next revision of the rules. The final definition wasn't ours or theirs. It was their process knowledge, made precise and repeatable in code.

From rules to learning, trained on the engineers' own labels

Once the rule-based detector had earned trust, the next question was whether a model could learn the engineers' judgment directly. The labeling method met the team where they were: an engineer marked skive windows the way he always had, by shading cells in Excel.

  • we parsed the workbook's cell fill colors programmatically, turning hundreds of hand-shaded rows into a per-position labeled training set, with no labeling tool and no new format for the team to learn;
  • a random-forest classifier learned to flag skive positions from each point's thickness and its differences to neighboring positions, later augmented with autoencoder features: dense and 1D-convolutional autoencoders trained on the full wire profiles, whose learned representations gave the classifier context about the whole profile shape rather than just a local window;
  • raw per-cell predictions were cleaned with a three-angle consensus rule: window boundaries only count where all three rotational angles agree, followed by edge trimming to remove stray predictions outside the consensus span;
  • predictions were written back into the workbook as a different shade next to the manual labels, so the engineer could audit the model exactly the way he'd created the training data;
  • we inspected the model rather than treating it as a black box: feature-importance rankings for the classifier, and visualizations of the autoencoder's convolutional kernels and layer activations to confirm the network was keying on the trough structure of real profiles.

A final anomaly screen closed the loop for quality control: scanning the finished workbooks for wires whose detected window contained unexpected gaps, and exporting those to a review list so engineer attention went straight to the wires that looked wrong.

Reports built for how engineers actually work

An algorithm nobody can inspect doesn't change a manufacturing process. The outputs were designed around the team's existing tools:

  • Highlighted Excel workbooks: the full measurement table with every detected skive-window cell shaded, so an engineer could verify any wire's window against the raw numbers in the tool they already lived in;
  • Multi-page PDF reports: one profile plot per wire with the detected window shaded and all dip cycles overlaid, making coating build-up visible at a glance;
  • An interactive Plotly explorer: dropdowns for fixture, wire, dip cycle, and angle to drill into any single measurement without touching code.

What it enabled

  • A consistent, objective skive-window definition across every wire, lot, and fixture, replacing per-engineer judgment calls;
  • Dip-to-dip comparisons anchored to the bare-wire baseline, quantifying how coating accumulates inside versus outside the window;
  • Per-angle views that expose asymmetry in coating around the wire's circumference;
  • Scalable review: full production lots screened automatically, with an anomaly report directing engineer attention to the wires that look wrong.

What this project demonstrates

  • Cross-functional collaboration: working shoulder to shoulder with process and manufacturing engineers, and treating their domain judgment as the spec the algorithm had to meet.
  • Translating tacit knowledge into code: turning "that edge doesn't look right" into explicit, auditable slope and tolerance rules.
  • Pragmatic machine learning: harvesting training labels from the engineers' own Excel shading, progressing from rules to a random forest to autoencoder representations only as each step proved its worth, and inspecting what the models learned rather than trusting them blindly.
  • Real-world data hygiene: diagnosing and repairing duplicate and mislabeled production records before drawing a single conclusion.
  • Meeting users in their tools: shipping results as shaded Excel sheets, PDF plot books, and an interactive explorer rather than expecting engineers to read code.
